Mamba2 ships with an 8×-bigger recurrent state than its predecessor. Cutting that state should be cheap. It isn't — the standard tools either dilute the state without making it smaller, lose the plot under stress, or demand more VRAM than an A100 has. We built a forward-pass-only state pruner for Mamba2 from a control-theory primitive and tracked every place it does and doesn't work.

By Michael Menezes & Anastasios Kyrillidis · Rice CS · May 2026

Between Mamba1 and Mamba2 the state-space dimension $N$ jumped from 16 to 128. For a 1.3-billion-parameter model, that pushed the recurrent state from about 12 MB to roughly 100 MB. The model got better at long-range temporal modelling and worse at autoregressive throughput — decode is now memory-bandwidth-bound, and the 100 MB sloshes through cache on every token.

The obvious move is to prune that state. The non-obvious part is that none of the standard pruning recipes do the job cleanly:

Unstructured weight pruners (SparseGPT, Wanda) zero out individual weights but leave activations dense. The recurrent state $\bm{H}_t$ stays fully populated; bandwidth doesn't drop. Magnitude pruning looks at the input/output projection weights $\bm{W}_B,\bm{W}_C$ and asks which channels have small norms. As we'll show, this is wrong about 41% of the time. Gradient-based pruning (Taylor scoring) is more accurate but needs to backprop through the whole graph — that's 45+ GB of VRAM for a 1.3B model and falls apart under length and scale stress.

We wanted something with magnitude pruning's price tag, Taylor's fidelity, and an honest answer when pushed to 70% sparsity. We called it GHOST — Grouped Hidden-state Output-aware Selection and Truncation.

41% Misclassification rate of magnitude pruning at 50% sparsity
The proxy failure Static weight magnitude is anti-correlated (Pearson $-0.19$) with what each channel actually does at runtime. Half the channels you'd save on weight grounds are inert. Half the ones you'd kill are doing real work.

Phantoms and corporeals

A Mamba2 hidden state is a coordinate in a recurrent memory bank. Each of Mamba2-1.3B's 6,144 hidden states ($48$ layers $\times$ $128$ states each) is fed by a row of $\bm{W}_B$ and read out by a column of $\bm{W}_C$. The natural — and naive — importance proxy is the product of those two weight norms.

We plotted that static score against the channel's dynamic energy across a calibration corpus, standardized both axes, and looked at the Pearson correlation. It's $-0.19$. Not anti-correlated enough for a clean inversion to fix it. Just… uncorrelated, leaning slightly the wrong way.

Two failure modes, two new vocabulary words. Phantom states — high activity, low weight norm, false negatives that magnitude pruning would kill. Corporeal states — large weights, low utilisation, false positives that magnitude pruning would keep. At 50% sparsity, magnitude pruning misclassifies 41.3% of the population, almost evenly split.

The metric you'd reach for is the metric that's wrong about half its calls. Anything you build on top of it inherits that error.

Forward-only physics Two forward passes through the calibration data is all GHOST needs. The score is a control-theoretic quantity, not a gradient. That's where the 15 GB vs 45+ GB asymmetry comes from.

The control-theory side door

There's a forty-year-old idea from system identification called balanced truncation (Moore, 1981). For a linear time-invariant system, you measure two things: how strongly the input drives each internal state (controllability), and how strongly each state drives the output (observability). The product of those two quantities — the Hankel singular values — ranks the states by how much they actually contribute to the input–output behaviour. Truncating the lowest of them is provably the best low-order approximation in operator norm.

Mamba2 is not linear time-invariant. The recurrence is input-dependent (selective), which is why — according to the orthodoxy — balanced truncation doesn't apply. We argue it does, with one substitution: instead of solving a Lyapunov equation for the Gramians, estimate them from a calibration corpus.

For a state channel $n$ in group $g$, define two empirical scalars at each timestep: the squared hidden state value $\bm{P}^{(g)}_{k,n,p}(t) = (\bm{H}_{t,h_k,n,p})^2$ (controllability proxy — how excited is this channel?) and the squared output projection $\bm{Q}^{(g)}_{n}(t) = (\bm{C}'_{t,g,n})^2$ (observability proxy — how loudly will it speak to the output?). GHOST's saliency is their average product, square-rooted to recover the dimensionality of a singular value:

$$ \bm{S}^{(g)}_n \;=\; \sqrt{\frac{1}{Z}\sum_{d=1}^{|\mathcal{D}_{\text{cal}}|}\sum_{t=1}^{L}\sum_{k=1}^{K}\sum_{p=1}^{P}\big(\bm{H}_{t|d,h_k,n,p}\big)^2\big(\bm{C}'_{t|d,g,n}\big)^2.} $$

There's a second, independent reading of the same expression: it's exactly the local mean-squared error you'd incur from setting that channel to zero. Minimise the saliency, minimise the local damage. The two perspectives — control-theoretic and loss-preservation — arrive at the same scoring rule from opposite directions.

Two operational details matter. First, GHOST pools across GQA groups within a layer before thresholding — complex groups keep more channels, redundant groups get hammered. No per-layer rate has to be tuned by hand. Second, GHOST runs sequentially down the model, recalibrating each layer's activations after pruning the previous one. That's what stops the "the layer below me changed under my feet" failure mode that haunts one-shot gradient methods.

At 50% sparsity Taylor wins on perplexity. That story flips at 70%, where Taylor jumps by a factor of 300; at 90% it goes to 4690. GHOST stays at 16.16 and 25.07. Taylor's collapse is structural — one-shot scoring on the full graph means the upstream pruning has invalidated the downstream gradients before the downstream pruning happens. Sequential recalibration is what saves GHOST.

Length generalisation

Calibrate at $L_{\text{cal}}=128$ tokens, evaluate up to $L_{\text{eval}}=2048$. The dense model and SparseGPT both improve with longer context (more bits). Magnitude and Random degrade gracefully. Taylor diverges to 1613 at $L_{\text{eval}}=2048$ — a Taylor score computed on short contexts plain doesn't transfer to long-range dependencies. GHOST tracks the dense curve.

Scaling

Five model sizes, 130M to 2.7B, all at 50% sparsity. Smaller models have less redundancy, so pruning bites harder. Taylor is unusable on the small end — $\sim 10^{16}$ on 130M, 12647 on 370M — before recovering at 1.3B and 2.7B. GHOST is the only structured method that's actually deployable across the entire range:

Out of distribution

A separate question: do the channels GHOST keeps look like English-text channels, or like Mamba2 channels? We calibrated on WikiText-2 (English text) and tested zero-shot on code (HumanEval) and math (MMLU Elementary Mathematics). Among structured methods, GHOST has the smallest in-domain vs out-of-domain gap, ties Taylor on code, and matches the dense baseline on the math task. The pruned channels are fundamentally important to the SSM, not just to next-word prediction.

Honest scope GHOST is not a free lunch. Pruning the recurrent state limits exact-match retrieval. Aggregate zero-shot stays within 2.5 points of dense; specific recall tasks pay more.

What we don't claim

Four limitations the paper states plainly, and we won't soft-pedal here.

Limitation 1 · Extreme sparsity

At 90%, GHOST degrades.

Perplexity climbs to 25 from a dense 13.17. The elbow plot in the appendix shows that 90% sparsity forces removal of highly utilised states — states that pass GHOST's controllability test. There is no scoring rule that survives a budget that small. We report the number anyway.

Limitation 2 · Small models

130M models suffer harder.

Mamba2-130M goes from 25.85 dense to 29.06 pruned at 50% — a 12% relative perplexity hit, versus 8% for the 1.3B model. Less representational redundancy, less room to give up. Pruning gets easier as you scale up; on toy models it is genuinely costly.

Limitation 3 · Exact recall

Lambada drops 14 points.

Compressing recurrent state limits exact-match retrieval — you can't memorise as many specific facts in less memory. We see a 14-point Lambada accuracy drop at 50% sparsity. Aggregate zero-shot performance stays within 2.5 points of the dense baseline, but the trade-off is real and is the reason we wouldn't use GHOST naively for retrieval-heavy applications.

Limitation 4 · Architecture-specific

This is a Mamba2 result.

GHOST exploits Mamba2's scalar-identity $\bm{A}$ and grouped-query (GQA) structure. The same control-theoretic principle should apply to S4, H3, Mamba1, hybrid Jamba-style models — but the specific Gramian approximations have to be re-derived. Drop-in transfer is not promised. (We do show one transfer experiment in the paper: Zamba2, a Mamba2+Transformer hybrid.)

What this is really about

The narrow win is a state pruner for Mamba2 that lets a 1.3-billion-parameter model fit on the kind of GPU sitting in academic labs, not just industrial clouds. The broader bet is that forward-only Gramian estimation is a general principle for identifying functionally-important channels in deep networks — one we expect to outlive this specific paper.

Two follow-ups we're already drafting. GHOST-Cache: a controllability $\times$ observability score for KV-cache row eviction in transformers, going head-to-head with H2O and SnapKV. Provable balanced-truncation bounds: closing the empirical–theoretical gap with operator-norm guarantees for the selective LTV regime. The first targets a transformer audience an order of magnitude bigger than the SSM one. The second turns the empirical scoring rule into a theorem.

For the GHOST paper itself, the right way to read it is as a worked example of a general claim. Forward passes carry more information about which channels do real work than the field has been giving them credit for. A back-of-the-envelope expectation, taken seriously, replaces an expensive Hessian. We expect to see versions of this argument show up in places we haven't written about yet.
